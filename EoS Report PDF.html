The relationship
Staff who feel more socially connected also tend to feel more comfortable speaking up. Connectedness scores notably higher than empowerment with less variation across staff.
Statistical significance
With 28 respondents, a p-value of 0.007 indicates the relationship is unlikely to be due to chance.
Practical read
Overall: Fostering stronger team connection may help staff feel more psychologically safe to voice differing opinions. The moderate r (0.50) suggests other factors beyond social connection also influence empowerment. This pattern holds across every subgroup — connectedness consistently exceeds empowerment for new, returning, campus, and remote staff alike.

New vs. returning: New staff score lower on both connectedness (3.44 vs. 3.95) and empowerment (3.00 vs. 3.74), with the empowerment gap being the larger of the two.

On campus vs. remote: Remote staff score slightly lower on connectedness (3.60 vs. 3.83) yet slightly higher on empowerment (3.60 vs. 3.48). With only 5 remote respondents this should be interpreted cautiously.

Open-Ended Responses
What is something Hollyhock, as an organisation, is doing well?
Responses to this question were notably positive and consistent. Staff across tenure levels point to the same core strengths: a culture that attracts and retains good people, food and physical spaces that create a sense of belonging, pride in the quality of guest programming, and a felt sense that the organization genuinely cares about the people who work there. The 2025 wage increase was the most frequently cited concrete action, mentioned spontaneously by new and returning staff alike.
Staff experience, care & culture
14 / 28 responses — new & returning
The strongest and most consistent theme. Returning staff describe a culture that has improved meaningfully over time; new staff arrive and immediately notice the care as something distinctive.
Wages & compensation
6 / 28 responses — mostly returning
The 2025 wage increase was mentioned spontaneously by six respondents. Returning staff credit it as recognition of their value; at least one new staff member also noticed the shift.
Guest experience & programming
6 / 28 responses — mostly returning
Staff take pride in the quality of what Hollyhock delivers to guests. New staff echo this around team culture and the guest experience without the historical comparison returning staff bring.
Food & physical spaces
6 / 28 responses — new & returning
Food is consistently named, aligning with its rank as the top perk. The garden, restaurant, and physical setting are framed as connective tissue for community.
Diversity, land stewardship & indigenous relations
3 / 28 responses — returning only
Named exclusively by returning staff, reflecting depth of relationship with place over time. These respondents frame land-based learning and indigenous partnerships as a core organizational strength.
Communication & transparency efforts
3 / 28 responses — new & returning
A smaller theme here, notable because communication recurs as the dominant improvement area in Q2. Where it works, staff notice and appreciate it.
What is something Hollyhock as an organisation could improve on?
Responses here were candid and often specific. Communication failures, training gaps, and workload pressure emerged as the clearest organizational signals — and notably, new staff named the same problems as returning staff on arrival, before accumulated context would explain the frustration. A small number of responses raise concerns that are lower frequency but higher stakes: conflict left unresolved, compensation inequity, and one detailed account of kitchen hygiene that warrants direct follow-up.
Communication & interdepartmental coordination
9 / 28 responses — new & returning
The single most common improvement theme. Returning staff describe it as a systemic cost in time, money, and institutional memory. New staff arrive and notice the gaps immediately, strengthening this signal considerably.
Training, onboarding & procedure documentation
6 / 28 responses — new & returning
New staff are notably more direct about onboarding gaps; returning staff frame it as professional development. Together they identify both ends of the training pipeline. Procedure manuals are called out specifically.
Staff consultation & collaborative decision-making
4 / 28 responses — returning only
All four responses came from returning staff, who have enough context to recognize when decisions are made without their input. The concern is about being consulted before, not informed after.
Workload, burnout & hustle culture
4 / 28 responses — new & returning
Returning staff describe burnout as a structural cycle; new staff name the culture directly. The new staff framing ("hustle/urgency culture") is the explicit label for what returning staff describe in behavioral terms.
Wages & compensation equity
4 / 28 responses — new & returning
Despite Q1 praise for the wage increase, some respondents still raise wage concerns. Returning staff focus on internal equity; new staff raise absolute wage levels.
Conflict resolution & staff housing
2 / 28 responses — returning only
Two returning staff raise conflict as inadequately resolved this season. Housing is named as a compounding factor, since problematic co-workers are also co-habitants.
Noted, not recurring
24/7 staffing coverage — new staff

Internship & hiring practices — returning staff
"I've noticed a growing emphasis on adopting certain American internship practices that seem to prioritize candidates from privileged or elite educational backgrounds — more like a branding exercise than one grounded in our core values."
Food safety & kitchen sanitation — returning staff
"Please improve sanitation and cleanliness standards in the kitchen. It's beyond unsanitary, with almost all cooks licking stirring/serving spoons while cooking. There seems to be zero cleanliness training. Please get sneeze guards for the buffet."
What does "organizational transparency" in a workplace mean for you?
Staff across both groups define transparency primarily as clear, timely communication about decisions and the reasoning behind them. Returning staff extend that definition further, adding psychological safety and protected feedback channels as essential components. New staff are more likely to frame it in operational terms — knowing their role, having access to organizational information. Where the groups converge, the definition is consistent: transparency means being brought into the "why," not just the "what."
Open, timely communication about decisions
12 / 24 responses — new & returning
The dominant definition. Staff understand transparency as leadership communicating clearly about decisions, especially those that affect staff themselves, with enough context to understand the reasoning, not just the outcome.
Psychological safety to speak up
5 / 24 responses — returning only
All responses came from returning staff. Transparency is not just top-down information flow — it requires conditions where staff can raise concerns and give feedback without fear of penalty. This connects directly to the empowerment gap in the quantitative data.
Wage & financial transparency
3 / 24 responses — new & returning
Both new and returning staff name financial and wage transparency as part of their definition. New staff frame it broadly; returning staff are more specific about SLT compensation and campus/remote wage bands.
Role & process clarity
3 / 24 responses — new staff only
A theme contributed entirely by new staff, who define transparency partly as knowing what their role requires and how things work — a more operational lens than the governance-focused definitions from returning staff.
Noted, not recurring
Calibrated openness — returning staff
"The staff are brought into the loop when it comes to change, planning, and direction. It sounds strange, but some transparency is good, too much can cause concern or be ineffective."
Organizational standing — returning staff
"That everyone is on the level with how the organisation is doing, what it is claiming to stand for and what it intends to become."
Signals Across the Dataset
Requires follow-upFood safety concern
One Q2 response contains detailed, specific allegations about kitchen hygiene practices. This is a single respondent and should not be treated as a widespread complaint. However, the specificity, urgency, and health/liability implications mean it should be investigated directly. It cannot be averaged away by its frequency in the data.
CultureBurnout is structural; hustle culture is its name
The quantitative data shows a 25-point gap in workload concerns between new and returning staff. The Q2 open text explains the mechanism: a seasonal cycle where burnout recovery eats into next-season planning. A new staff respondent names this directly as hustle/urgency culture and offers concrete countermeasures.
CultureReturning staff apply a more critical lens to transparency
New staff define transparency as role and process clarity. Returning staff extend it to psychological safety and the ability to give feedback without fear. The most pointed response in the dataset came from a returning staff member — consistent with empowerment scores not improving with tenure.
StructuralCommunication is the central organizational concern
Communication problems surface in every question and from both staff groups. In Q1 it appears as a strength. In Q2 it is the most common improvement theme (9/28). In Q3, timely communication is the dominant definition of transparency. The new/returning convergence rules out a tenure-specific frustration.
StructuralNew staff name the same problems as returning staff, but faster
New staff are not yet habituated to organizational friction points, so their observations are unfiltered. They independently flag communication gaps, onboarding inadequacy, and hustle culture. When both groups arrive at the same diagnosis from different vantage points, the signal is robust.
StructuralOnboarding is a specific and actionable gap
New staff are considerably more direct about onboarding than returning staff, who frame the issue as professional development. New staff specifically name: longer onboarding duration, procedure manuals by role, and better use of existing staff skills and knowledge.
StructuralWage progress is recognized, but equity and transparency gaps remain
The 2025 wage increase drew spontaneous praise from six respondents in Q1. Yet Q2 and Q3 both surface wage concerns: bodyworker pay, remote vs. campus wage bands, SLT salary visibility, and for new staff, absolute wage levels. Appreciation and concern coexist in the same dataset.

What does "organizational transparency" in a workplace mean for you?
  1. It means that decisions are made with appropriate levels of consultation, and that people impacted are informed in a timely manner and also in an effective way so they actually receive the information that allows them to understand how their leadership operates.
  2. That the roles, tasks and responsibilities are clear.
  3. Openness and honesty with the management details and structure of the organization.
  4. Staff understand what the organisation is doing and why and feel confident to support organisational decision making and direction in the community.
  5. When something should be confidential it should stay confidential so people don't start gossiping.
  6. To have senior managers and executives be completely open and honest with staff members.
  7. Clear and timely communication around decisions that affect onsite staff and the campus.
  8. Sharing facts rather than smooth talking.
  9. Clear communication.
  10. The ability to communicate openly when things are going well, but also in challenging situations — things that are unfortunately lacking, because management is operating out of fear and scarcity, and this vibration trickles down to everyone.
  11. Honest and open communication, room for questions and feedback.
  12. Understanding how and why decisions are made.
  13. Things are open where possible.
  14. Just being clear on the goals of the organization and of the roles within it.
  15. Wage transparency. Equal opportunity to new and open positions both internally and publicly. Equal opportunity to perks. Managerial decisions, policy changes, budget allocations shared within the organization and accessible to all.
  16. Being able to openly dialogue challenging or vulnerable topics without fear of being penalized or dismissed.
  17. The staff are brought into the loop when it comes to change, planning, and direction. It sounds strange, but some transparency is good, too much can cause concern or be ineffective.
  18. Organizational transparency in a workplace means that the SLT and Board are willing to communicate openly with staff about strategic plans, organizational changes, goals, intentions, and changes to process. I think it also means that there is an open channel for feedback where staff can communicate without fear of being penalized. And that their privacy around such feedback is respected and protected.
  19. When an organization is open and honest about the company's goals, mission, vision, strategy, financials, challenges, etc. with all partners, including staff — and where everyone is accountable for their actions, trust is built, and communication is prioritized.
  20. Openly sharing information with employees, such as decisions, processes and performance.
  21. Not sure.
  22. That everyone is on the level with how the organisation is doing, what it is claiming to stand for and what it intends to become.
  23. SLT and upper management wage transparency for all staff. Remote and campus wage bands and how and why they differ. Transparency when making large decisions that will affect the whole organization and everyone's jobs.
  24. To me, this means being open with the organization's aims, challenges, and plans.
