Rewarding: staff themes
Open responses echoed the pre-defined options, with staff highlighting the sense of peace and belonging the natural setting provides. Several responses mentioned pride in the team’s commitment to quality and staff experience as a core motivating force.
Challenging: staff themes
Open responses highlighted health benefits gaps, communication and operational leadership concerns, and the physical demands of some roles. Some staff noted concern about witnessing colleagues burn out.
Key insights
Coworkers are the universal motivator: Selected by 89% of new and 74% of returning staff, the most stable signal across all groups and tenure lengths.

Mission resonates most with new staff and fades with tenure: 56% of first-season staff cite the leadership education mission as motivating, dropping to 25% among 8+ season staff. Long-tenured staff may need active re-engagement around organizational purpose.

Workload accumulates: Only 25% of new staff flag workload as a challenge, compared to 53% of returning staff. This gap mirrors the burnout concerns in open responses and the empowerment gap seen in the connectedness data.

The relationship
Staff who feel more socially connected also tend to feel more comfortable speaking up. Both scores sit above the midpoint, but connectedness scores notably higher than empowerment with less variation across staff.
Statistical significance
Even though there are only 28 respondents, a p-value of 0.007 indicates the relationship is unlikely to be due to chance.
Practical read
Overall relationship: Fostering stronger team connection may help staff feel more psychologically safe to voice differing opinions. However, the moderate r (0.50) also suggests other factors beyond social connection are influencing empowerment. This pattern holds across every subgroup examined: connectedness consistently exceeds empowerment for new, returning, campus, and remote staff alike. The only near-exception is remote staff, where the two scores are equal (3.60).

New vs. returning staff: New staff score lower on both connectedness (3.44 vs. 3.95) and empowerment (3.00 vs. 3.74), with the empowerment gap being the larger of the two. This suggests that early integration, building social bonds and feeling established within the team, may be especially important for psychological safety.

On campus vs. remote staff: Campus and remote staff score similarly overall, but with a notable reversal: remote staff score slightly lower on connectedness (3.60 vs. 3.83) yet slightly higher on empowerment (3.60 vs. 3.48). With only 5 remote respondents this should be interpreted cautiously, but it may suggest that remote staff experience fewer in-group social dynamics that can sometimes suppress differing opinions.
Open-Ended Responses
What is something Hollyhock, as an organisation, is doing well?
Responses to this question were notably positive and consistent. Staff across tenure levels point to the same core strengths: a culture that attracts and retains good people, food and physical spaces that create a sense of belonging, pride in the quality of guest programming, and a felt sense that the organization genuinely cares about the people who work there. The 2025 wage increase was the most frequently cited concrete action, mentioned spontaneously by new and returning staff alike. Where themes appear in both groups independently, the signal is stronger — these are not just returning staff nostalgia but qualities that new staff recognize on arrival.

What is something Hollyhock as an organisation could improve on?
Responses here were candid and often specific. Communication failures, training gaps, and workload pressure emerged as the clearest organizational signals — and notably, new staff named the same problems as returning staff on arrival, before accumulated context would explain the frustration. A small number of responses raise concerns that are lower frequency but higher stakes: conflict left unresolved, compensation inequity, and one detailed account of kitchen hygiene that warrants direct follow-up regardless of how many people raised it.

What does "organizational transparency" in a workplace mean for you?
Staff across both groups define transparency primarily as clear, timely communication about decisions and the reasoning behind them. Returning staff extend that definition further, adding psychological safety and protected feedback channels as essential components. New staff are more likely to frame it in operational terms — knowing their role, having access to organizational information. Where the groups converge, the definition is consistent: transparency means being brought into the "why," not just the "what."

Cross-Question Synthesis
Signals Across the Dataset
Requires follow-upFood safety concern
›
One Q2 response contains detailed, specific allegations about kitchen hygiene practices. This is a single respondent and should not be treated as a widespread complaint. However, the specificity, urgency, and health/liability implications mean it should be investigated directly. It cannot be averaged away by its frequency in the data.
CultureBurnout is structural; hustle culture is its name
›
The quantitative data shows a 25-point gap in workload concerns between new and returning staff. The Q2 open text explains the mechanism: a seasonal cycle where burnout recovery eats into next-season planning. A new staff respondent names this directly as hustle/urgency culture and offers concrete countermeasures. The two framings together make a stronger case for structural intervention than either would alone.
CultureReturning staff apply a more critical lens to transparency
›
In Q3, new staff define transparency in terms of role and process clarity. Returning staff include that, but extend it to psychological safety and the ability to give feedback without fear. The most pointed response in the dataset came from a returning staff member. This is consistent with empowerment scores not improving with tenure in the quantitative data.
StructuralCommunication is the central organizational concern
›
Communication problems surface in every question and from both new and returning staff. In Q1 it appears as a strength (the Hollyhock Sun, feedback channels). In Q2 it is the most common improvement theme (9/28 responses). In Q3, transparent and timely communication is the dominant definition of what organizational transparency even means. The new/returning convergence rules out a tenure-specific frustration.
StructuralNew staff name the same problems as returning staff, but faster
›
New staff are not yet habituated to the organization friction points, so their observations are fresh and unfiltered. They independently flag communication gaps, onboarding inadequacy, and hustle culture -- the same structural concerns that returning staff describe with years of accumulated context. When both groups arrive at the same diagnosis from different vantage points, the signal is robust.
StructuralOnboarding is a specific and actionable gap
›
New staff are considerably more direct about onboarding than returning staff, who frame the issue as professional development. New staff specifically name: longer onboarding duration, procedure manuals by role, and better use of existing staff skills and knowledge. These are discrete, implementable asks rather than general sentiment.
StructuralWage progress is recognized, but equity and transparency gaps remain
›
The 2025 wage increase drew spontaneous praise from six respondents in Q1, from both new and returning staff. Yet Q2 and Q3 both surface wage concerns: bodyworker pay, remote vs. campus wage bands, SLT salary visibility, and for new staff, absolute wage levels. Leadership should not read Q1 praise as full closure -- the appreciation and the concern coexist in the same dataset.
