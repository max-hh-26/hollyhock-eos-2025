Rewarding: staff themes
Written responses echoed the pre-defined options, with staff highlighting the sense of peace and belonging the natural setting provides. Several mentioned pride in the team’s commitment to quality, and named the overall staff experience as a core motivating force.
Challenging: staff themes
Written responses highlighted health benefits gaps, communication and operational leadership concerns, and the physical demands of some roles. Some staff noted concern about witnessing colleagues burn out.
Key insights
Coworkers are the primary motivator: Selected by 89% of new and 74% of returning staff, the most consistent finding across all groups and tenure lengths.

Mission resonates most with new staff and fades with tenure: 56% of first-season staff cite the leadership education mission as motivating, dropping to 25% among 8+ season staff. Long-tenured staff may benefit from active re-engagement around organizational purpose.

Workload accumulates: Only 25% of new staff flag workload as a challenge, compared to 53% of returning staff. This gap is consistent with burnout concerns in written responses and the empowerment patterns seen in the connectedness data.

The relationship
Staff who feel more socially connected also tend to feel more comfortable speaking up. Both scores sit above the midpoint, though connectedness scores higher than empowerment with less variation across staff. This gap appears to be driven more by tenure than by work location. New and returning staff show a larger spread on both measures than campus and remote staff do.
Statistical significance
Even with only 28 respondents, a p-value of 0.007 indicates the relationship is unlikely to be due to chance.
Practical read
Overall relationship: Fostering stronger team connection may help staff feel more psychologically safe to voice differing opinions. Social connection and empowerment are related, though one does not fully predict the other; other factors beyond team bonds are likely influencing how comfortable staff feel speaking up. Connectedness consistently exceeds empowerment across every subgroup examined (new, returning, campus, and remote staff alike), with the only near-exception being remote staff, where the two scores are equal (3.60).

New vs. returning staff: New staff score lower on both connectedness (3.44 vs. 3.95) and empowerment (3.00 vs. 3.74), with the empowerment gap being the larger of the two. Early integration, building social bonds and feeling established within the team, may be especially important for psychological safety.

On campus vs. remote staff: Campus and remote staff score similarly overall, but remote staff score slightly lower on connectedness (3.60 vs. 3.83) yet slightly higher on empowerment (3.60 vs. 3.48). With only 5 remote respondents this should be interpreted cautiously, but it may suggest that remote staff experience fewer in-group social dynamics that can sometimes suppress differing opinions.
Open-Ended Responses
What is something Hollyhock, as an organisation, is doing well?
Responses were notably positive and consistent. Staff pointed most often to the quality of the people and culture, the physical setting and food, and the guest programming experience. The 2025 wage increase was the most frequently cited concrete action, mentioned spontaneously across different tenures and roles. The consistency is meaningful; these are qualities that new staff recognize on arrival, without the benefit of historical comparison.

What is something Hollyhock as an organisation could improve on?
Responses were candid and often specific. Interestingly, new staff identified many of the same structural issues as returning staff, suggesting the concerns aren't based solely in accumulated frustration. A small number of responses raise lower-frequency but higher-stakes concerns, including conflict left unresolved, compensation inequity, and one detailed account of kitchen hygiene that warrants direct follow-up regardless of how many people raised it.

What does "organizational transparency" in a workplace mean for you?
Staff across both groups converge on a shared core definition: transparency means being brought into the "why," not just the "what." Returning staff extend that definition to psychological safety and protected feedback channels, while new staff frame it more operationally, in terms of role clarity and access to organizational information. The consistency of the core definition across tenure levels makes it a reliable indicator of what staff expect from leadership.

Cross-Question Synthesis
Signals Across the Dataset
Requires follow-upFood safety concern
›
One response contains detailed, specific allegations about kitchen hygiene practices. This is a single respondent and should not be treated as a widespread complaint. However, the specificity, urgency, and health/liability implications mean it should be investigated directly. It cannot be averaged away by its frequency in the data.
CultureBurnout is structural; hustle culture is its name
›
Returning staff are considerably more likely than new staff to flag workload as a concern, which may have a connection to the lack of improvement in empowerment scores with tenure. Staff describe the pattern as a seasonal burnout cycle; those newer to the organization name it directly as hustle/urgency culture and identify concrete countermeasures. Together the framings point toward a structural pattern that builds with time.
StructuralCulture and infrastructure are out of step
›
Staff consistently named Hollyhock's human dimension as its greatest strength, from the culture and care to the quality of people the organization attracts and retains. Coworkers were cited as a motivating factor by nearly 80% of respondents across all tenure levels. The most common improvement areas, however, are almost entirely structural and operational. Together they describe an organization with a strong cultural core and an infrastructure that is still catching up.
StructuralCommunication is the central organizational concern
›
Communication surfaces as both a strength and a concern across the dataset. Staff praised specific communication efforts (the Hollyhock Sun, feedback channels) while simultaneously naming communication failures as the most common improvement area (9 of 28 responses). When asked what organizational transparency means to them, timely and clear communication was the most common definition. The year-over-year transparency data indicates the share of returning staff who felt transparency had improved softened between 2024 and 2025. New and returning staff converge on this issue across every section, making accumulated frustration alone unlikely to be the culprit.
StructuralNew staff name the same problems as returning staff, but faster
›
New staff are not yet habituated to the organization's friction points, so their observations are fresh and unfiltered. They independently name many of the same structural concerns as returning staff (communication gaps, onboarding inadequacy, and hustle culture), despite having far less accumulated context. The empowerment data reflects a similar pattern. New staff score lower overall than returning staff, but empowerment within the returning group tends to decline with tenure. The concerns named early do not appear to resolve with time.
StructuralOnboarding is a specific and actionable gap
›
Training and onboarding was the second most commonly named improvement area, cited in 6 of 28 responses. New staff were considerably more direct about the gaps than returning staff, who tend to frame the same issue as professional development. New staff specifically named longer onboarding duration, procedure manuals by role, and better use of existing staff skills and knowledge.
StructuralWage progress is recognized, but equity and transparency gaps remain
›
The 2025 wage increase drew spontaneous praise from six respondents, from both new and returning staff. Alongside that recognition, ongoing concerns surfaced around bodyworker pay, remote vs. campus wage bands, SLT salary visibility, and for new staff, absolute wage levels. The appreciation and the concern coexist in the same dataset.
